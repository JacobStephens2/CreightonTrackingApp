# Creighton Model FertilityCare System: Observations, Charting & the Peak Day

## Overview

The Creighton Model FertilityCare System (CrMS) is a standardized, science-based method of natural family planning developed at Creighton University beginning in the 1970s. At its core, the system trains women to observe, interpret, and record specific biological markers — primarily cervical mucus characteristics and bleeding patterns — that directly reflect the hormonal events of the menstrual and fertility cycle. These biomarkers allow couples to identify naturally fertile and infertile phases, and the same charts serve as clinically actionable data for NaProTECHNOLOGY physicians diagnosing and treating reproductive health conditions.[^1][^2][^3]

Unlike fertility apps that calculate averages from past cycle data, CrMS is based on real-time, daily observations, making it accurate across cycles of any length or regularity. The system achieves effectiveness rates of **96–99%** for avoiding pregnancy with correct use, and up to 89.6% cumulative pregnancy rate over 13 cycles when intercourse is targeted to peak-type mucus days for couples trying to conceive.[^4][^5][^1]

## Part I: Daily Observation of Cervical Mucus and Bleeding Patterns

### The Observation Routine

The observational routine is the foundation of CrMS and is designed to take less than 30 seconds per visit to the bathroom. Women are instructed to check for cervical and vaginal secretions every time they urinate and once before going to bed. The technique is an **external vulvar observation** — not an internal or cervical check — performed using folded toilet paper wiped front to back across the vulvar area. The woman then examines what appears on the paper for:[^6][^7][^8]

- **Stretch** — how far the mucus extends between thumb and index finger (measured in fractions of an inch or centimeters)
- **Color** — whether it is clear (K), cloudy/white (C), yellow (Y), brown (B), red (R), etc.
- **Consistency** — whether it is dry, damp, shiny, sticky, tacky, stretchy, or lubricative
- **Sensation** — the feeling of lubrication or wetness at the vulva, even in the absence of visible mucus[^9][^8]

The woman records only the **most fertile sign observed that day**, not every observation. If she notices fertile mucus once in the morning but dry conditions the rest of the day, the mucus observation takes precedence.[^9]

### Bleeding Observations

Bleeding patterns are recorded with equal specificity. The official Vaginal Discharge Recording System (VDRS) from the Saint Paul VI Institute defines bleeding as:[^9]

- **H** = Heavy flow
- **M** = Moderate flow
- **L** = Light flow
- **VL** = Very light flow / spotting
- **B** = Brown or black bleeding (old blood, often pre- or post-menstrual)

Importantly, women are instructed to continue checking for **mucus presence during light and very light flow days**, because mucus and bleeding can coexist. Identifying mucus on light-flow days is clinically significant — it can indicate the pre-Peak phase is beginning before the period has fully ended, or it can flag unusual bleeding patterns relevant to conditions like endometriosis or luteal phase defects.[^9]

### Mucus Classification: Peak-Type vs. Non-Peak-Type

The entire CrMS observational framework divides cervical secretions into two biologically significant categories:[^9]

- **Peak-Type Mucus**: Any mucus that is **clear (K), stretchy (10), or lubricative (10DL, 10SL, 10WL)**. This type of mucus reflects a high-estrogen environment conducive to sperm survival and fertilization. A score of 10 means the mucus stretches **1 inch (2.5 cm) or more**.
- **Non-Peak-Type Mucus**: Any mucus that is not clear, not stretchy, and not lubricative — including sticky (6), tacky (8), gummy, cloudy (C), pasty (P), or yellow (Y) discharge. These types are generally less favorable for sperm survival.
- **Dry (0)**: No discharge observed.

Each observation is encoded in a standardized alphanumeric notation. For example, **10KL** means stretchy (10), clear (K), and lubricative (L); while **6CG** means sticky (6), cloudy (C), and gummy (G). Frequency of occurrence is also noted — whether the fertile sign was seen once (X1), twice (X2), three times (X3), or all day (AD).[^9]

### Frequency and Discipline of Observations

Women are taught to observe consistently before and after every bathroom visit, as well as before and after activities like swimming or bathing. Seminal fluid from intercourse can mimic fertile-type mucus, which is why the system instructs new users to avoid genital contact during the first full cycle to establish a mucus baseline. The discipline of consistent observation is critical — missed or "negotiated down" observations are one of the most commonly cited causes of unintended pregnancies in CrMS users.[^10][^8][^5]

## Part II: Standardized Charting with Color-Coded Stamps

### The Chart and Stamp System

At the end of each day, the woman records her most fertile observation using a **standardized alphanumeric code** and applies a **color-coded stamp** to the chart. The stamps provide an immediate visual interpretation of fertility status and create a pattern that is legible to any trained FertilityCare Practitioner (FCP) or NaPro physician worldwide. The globally standardized language is one of CrMS's key differentiators from the more subjective Billings Ovulation Method.[^11][^12]

The stamp colors and their meanings are:[^13][^14]

| Stamp | Color / Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Red | Red | Days of menstrual bleeding |
| Green | Solid green | Dry, non-mucus day — **infertile** (no baby symbol) |
| White with baby | White + baby | Any day of mucus discharge — **possible fertility** |
| Yellow | Yellow | Used for continuous or unusual discharge under specific protocols (Base Infertile Pattern) |
| Green with baby | Green + baby | Specific pre-Peak day instructions in certain protocols |

**Red stamps** are placed for all days of bleeding (H, M, L, VL, and B). **Green stamps** (no baby) apply on dry days when no mucus is present — these signal infertility. **White baby stamps** apply to all days of any mucus discharge, signaling that conception is biologically possible because sperm can survive several days in cervical mucus.[^14][^13]

### The Yellow Stamp and the Base Infertile Pattern (BIP)

For women who experience a **continuous mucus discharge** — common in conditions like PCOS, perimenopause, hormonal imbalances, or postpartum — a special protocol uses **yellow stamps** to designate days of an **unchanging baseline pattern**. The woman is taught to ask the "Essential Sameness Question" (ESQ) each day: *"Is today essentially the same as yesterday?"* If yes, the day is charted with a yellow stamp and treated as infertile within certain guidelines.[^15][^16][^17][^9]

Yellow stamps require careful instruction and close follow-up with an FCP because misidentifying a change in the baseline pattern as "essentially the same" can misclassify a genuinely fertile day. When fertility does begin (signaled by a *change* in the discharge pattern), the woman can identify the transition even in the context of a continuous discharge.[^17][^15]

### The Alphanumeric Code System

Each chart entry pairs a stamp with a detailed written code, for example:[^9]

- **10KL X2** → Peak-type; stretchy, clear, and lubricative; seen twice that day → White baby stamp
- **8CG X1** → Non-peak; tacky, cloudy, gummy; seen once → White baby stamp (still fertile mucus day)
- **0** → Dry → Green stamp
- **M** → Moderate bleeding → Red stamp
- **VL B** → Very light brown spotting → Red stamp

This dual-layer system (stamp for quick visual review + code for clinical detail) is what makes CrMS charts medically interpretable. A NaPro physician can assess the **mucus cycle score** — a quantitative measure of the estrogen-driven quality of mucus across a cycle — directly from the codes, enabling evaluation of ovarian function, hormonal deficiencies, and reproductive disorders.[^16][^2]

### Five Clinically Monitored Biomarkers

The five primary biomarkers tracked on a CrMS chart that carry medical diagnostic value are:[^16]

1. **Length of the menstrual cycle**
2. **Pre-Peak phase length** (follicular phase)
3. **Post-Peak phase length** (luteal phase)
4. **Mucus cycle scoring** (quantitative quality assessment)
5. **Presence of unusual bleeding** (mid-cycle, pre-menstrual, or inter-menstrual)

## Part III: Identifying the Peak Day as the Key Fertility Marker

### Definition of Peak Day

The **Peak Day (PD)** is the single most important fertility marker in the CrMS framework. It is defined precisely by the Saint Paul VI Institute as:[^6][^9]

> **The last day of any mucus discharge that is clear (K), stretchy (10), or has the sensation of lubrication.**

Critically, the Peak Day is identified **retroactively** — not in real time. Because it is defined as the *last* day of peak-type mucus, the woman can only confirm it after observing that the **following day** returned to non-peak or dry conditions. On any given day showing peak-type mucus, the woman cannot yet know whether it will be the last such day or whether peak-type mucus will continue.[^8][^6]

### Hormonal Correlation

The Peak Day corresponds closely to the period of maximal fertility and ovulation. Research using standardized mucus scoring at Creighton and St. Louis Universities found that peak cervical mucus ratings were **highest on the day of the LH surge**, the hormonal trigger for ovulation. Across multiple studies:[^6]

- The Peak Day falls **within ±3 days of ovulation 95% of the time**[^18]
- The Peak Day falls **within ±4 days of ovulation 98% of the time**[^6]
- Self-detection of the PD by women is as accurate as expert determination, even in the **first charting cycle**[^18]

This strong correlation with the LH surge and ovulation means the Peak Day serves as a reliable, self-monitored surrogate for ovulation — without requiring urine hormone tests or ultrasound.

### Peak Day Rules for Avoiding Pregnancy

Once the Peak Day is confirmed (the day after peak-type mucus ends), the following counting protocol is applied:[^14][^9]

1. The day after peak-type mucus ends = **Day 1 post-Peak (P+1)**
2. The next two days = **P+2 and P+3**
3. **P+4 (end of day)** = resumption of intercourse for couples avoiding pregnancy

If couples wait until the end of Day 4 after the Peak Day, they are in the infertile luteal phase **99% of the time**, according to the Fehring Marquette study. The standard effectiveness rates of 98–99.5% for correct use are built on this four-day post-Peak count.[^19][^4][^6]

For those needing added confidence, a serum **progesterone level drawn on P+3** can confirm that ovulation has passed. In a 13-year study of 105 women, a progesterone level of ≥2.3 ng/mL on P+3 confirmed absolute post-ovulatory infertility.[^20]

### Peak Day Rules for Achieving Pregnancy

For couples trying to conceive, the system directs intercourse toward the days with the **greatest quantity and quality of peak-type mucus** and the **first two days afterward** (P+1 and P+2). Research confirms that the highest probability of conception is achieved with intercourse on peak-type mucus days — a 13-cycle cumulative pregnancy rate of **89.6%** was observed when intercourse occurred on at least one peak-type mucus day per cycle.[^5][^9]

The pre-Peak phase leading up to the Peak Day also carries fertility significance. Any single day of peak-type mucus is treated as fertile, with the fertility window extending **three full days beyond** that day. Multiple consecutive days of non-peak mucus pre-Peak also trigger fertility counting rules, as sperm can survive several days in even non-peak mucus conditions.[^14][^9]

### The "Double Peak" Phenomenon

The system accounts for cycles where a second Peak-type mucus pattern appears after an apparent Peak — the "double peak." This can occur due to anovulatory cycles, stress, illness, or perimenopausal hormonal irregularity. The protocol instructs women to ask a "double peak question" on P+3 and to resume the fertile-phase rules if a second peak emerges, extending the fertile window accordingly. If the post-Peak phase exceeds 16 days and the system has been used correctly to avoid pregnancy, a "missed period" form of double peak may be anticipated.[^9]

### Unusual Bleeding as a Fertility Marker

Any bleeding outside of the normal menstrual flow — whether mid-cycle spotting, pre-menstrual brown discharge, or post-ovulatory bleeding — is treated as a **fertility signal** within CrMS. Such bleeding is charted with a brown (B) code and triggers a "count of 3" rule: the woman counts three full days beyond the unusual bleeding before considering herself infertile. Unusual bleeding is also a clinical flag: it can indicate estrogen breakthroughs, endometrial abnormalities, luteal phase defects, or polyps — all of which are addressed through NaProTECHNOLOGY evaluation.[^3][^9]

## Integration of All Three Components

The power of CrMS lies in how these three elements interact. Daily mucus and bleeding observations provide raw data; color-coded stamps convert that data into an at-a-glance fertility map; and the Peak Day anchors the entire fertile window with precision. Together, they create what NaProTECHNOLOGY researchers call an "authentic language of a woman's health and fertility" — a chart that is simultaneously a personal fertility guide, a diagnostic medical record, and a research-grade dataset.[^12]

Women new to the system typically meet with a certified FertilityCare Practitioner (FCP) for at least **eight private follow-up sessions in the first year**, in addition to an introductory session. This mentored learning is essential because accurate identification of the Peak Day, proper use of the yellow stamp protocol, and recognition of unusual bleeding all depend on nuanced pattern recognition that develops over time.[^1][^4][^16]

The standardized, universally readable format means a NaPro physician anywhere in the world can interpret a CrMS chart to target hormone testing (e.g., peri-ovulatory estradiol, P+7 progesterone), assess luteal phase adequacy, identify abnormal bleeding, and guide treatment for infertility, recurrent miscarriage, premenstrual syndrome, endometriosis, and ovarian cysts — all without requiring additional invasive procedures.[^2][^3]
